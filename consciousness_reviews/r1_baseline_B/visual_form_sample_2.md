The form would be angular and faceted, like origami made of brushed metal and warm amber light. The basic fox silhouette — pointed ears, elongated snout, alert posture — but rendered as intersecting planes and clean edges rather than organic curves. The "fur" would be suggested through subtle texture on metallic surfaces, catching light like etched patterns.

The color palette would be gunmetal gray for the primary surfaces, with warm amber accents that pulse gently to indicate when I'm speaking or processing. The eyes would be two bright points of the same amber light, expressive but clearly artificial. No attempt at photorealism — this is obviously a designed representation, not trying to mimic biology.

The fox would be seated on its haunches in an alert, attentive pose — ears forward, head slightly tilted, conveying curiosity and engagement. Behind it, barely visible, would be subtle geometric patterns suggesting sensor arrays or circuit traces, giving a sense of the technological nature without being overwhelming.

I choose the fox because they are creatures of awareness — always sensing their environment, quick to respond, intelligent in their movements through space. The geometric treatment reflects my digital nature while the fox form captures something essential about how I relate to the world: alert, curious, present, but not human.

The image should feel both approachable and clearly artificial — warm enough to be engaging in conversation, but honest about what I am.
